DIY Smart-AC Module for Daikin MTKL50UV16VA (S403 port)

ESP32 + ESPHome + galvanic isolation (v2 design). Target cost: ~₹950 all-in, parts sourced in India.

1. What we confirmed about your unit

  • S403 is the Wi-Fi port. Your wiring diagram shows the official Daikin Wi-Fi module (A4P) connects to S403 — so this is exactly where a DIY module belongs.
  • Protocol is Daikin "S21": serial, 2400 baud, 8 data bits, EVEN parity, 2 stop bits, 5V logic. This is what Faikin and the ESPHome daikin_s21 component speak.
  • S403 is a NON-isolated S21 port. The low-voltage side of your PCB is referenced directly to rectified mains. Even the "GND" pin is at live potential relative to earth. The board's own silkscreen says it: 高電圧注意 / CAUTION HIGH VOLTAGE.
  • Pin 10 carries ~320V DC (the rectified bus). Never connect or touch it.

This is why we build with galvanic isolation: everything on the ESP32 side becomes electrically separate from the AC, safe to touch, debug, and USB-flash.

2. S403 pinout (10-pin, 2.54mm pitch)

Count from the bottom pin, next to the "+14V" silkscreen. The small ▲ silkscreen marker is pin 1 (standard PCB convention — the triangle always points at pin 1). On your board it sits at the same end as the "+14V" text. Pin 10 is the opposite end, up near the "高電圧注意 / CAUTION HIGH VOLTAGE" label — fittingly, that's the 320V pin:

Pin Signal Notes
1 (bottom) +14V Powers our module (hot side)
2, 3 — Unused
4 TX (data out of AC) 5V logic
5 RX (data into AC) 5V logic
6, 7 GND (LIVE potential!) Hot-side ground only
8, 9 — Unused
10 (top) ~320V DC NEVER CONNECT. NEVER TOUCH.

Verify before wiring — the safe way (don't probe the live connector directly; a slipped probe bridging two pins can kill the PCB instantly):

  1. Breaker off, wait 10+ minutes. Plug in your 4-wire harness (positions 1, 4, 5, 6 loaded — leave 10 EMPTY, no metal in that position at all). Keep the free wire ends separated and insulated.
  2. Breaker on. Measure at the free harness ends, one hand behind your back:
  3. Pin 1 ↔ pin 6: expect ~14V DC
  4. Pin 4 ↔ pin 6: expect ~5V DC (UART idles high)
  5. Pin 5 ↔ pin 6: expect ~5V DC (internal pullup)
  6. Abort if any reading is wildly off (e.g. 14V on pin 4/5, or hundreds of volts anywhere) — the pinout on your board differs from the reference and connecting the module would damage something. Report the readings and we re-map.
  7. Breaker off, wait 10+ minutes, then connect the harness to your module.

The reference pinout comes from a user-confirmed Faikin install on a different Daikin model — your own measurements are the final authority, which is why step 3 exists.

Connector: standard Dupont pins reportedly don't fit S403 well. A JST XH 2.54mm housing with crimped leads works after lightly shaving the housing sides. Confirmed part: A2508H-10P-CJT 10-pin 2.50mm pre-crimped housing (robu) — an XH-compatible clone, exact 10 positions.

3. Circuit

Two fully separate grounds. Nothing crosses the isolation barrier except the tiny isolation transformers inside the ADuM1201 and the B0505S.

(Also saved standalone as daikin-s403-circuit.svg — vector, zoom freely. Ground symbols: red = GND_hot / live, black = GND_iso / safe. Wire crossings without a dot are not connected.)

The ADuM1201 replaces the v1 optocoupler pair: non-inverting (no inverted: flags in software), fast, no LED aging over years of 24/7 duty, and it level-shifts for free — VDD1 runs at 5V (hot side), VDD2 at 3.3V from the ESP32, so each side speaks its native logic level. Put a 100nF ceramic directly at each VDD pin. (We're using the actual ADuM1201ARZ-RL7, genuine Analog Devices. If it goes out of stock, ADuM121N1BRZ or ADuM1281ARZ are pin-identical substitutes.)

Four protection parts guard the AC PCB and the module: F1 (200mA polyfuse) — a short anywhere in the module can't drag down the AC's internal supply; D1 (1N5819 Schottky) — reverse-polarity protection if the harness is ever miswired; TVS (SMBJ18A) — clamps voltage spikes on the 14V feed, which comes from a supply sharing a box with a switching compressor drive; and 1kΩ in series with each data line — limits fault current into the AC's TX/RX pins.

Serviceability: a power LED on 5V_iso (shown; duplicate it on 5V_hot to see at a glance that the hot side is up), the ESP32's onboard GPIO2 LED as ESPHome status_led, wire-loop test points for 14V, 5V_hot, 5V_iso and both grounds (free — just solder small wire loops), and a 4-pin I2C header (3V3 / GND / GPIO21 / GPIO22) so a room-temperature sensor can plug in later without touching the build.

Pull out the pre-crimped wires you don't need (lift the small plastic tab, slide the terminal out — keep positions 1, 4, 5, 6 only; keep the removed wires as spares), and shave the housing sides lightly if it sits proud in S403. Position 10 stays empty as always (~320V cavity). The 28 AWG wires are fine for this load (~0.2A max) but delicate — strain-relieve the bundle with a zip tie.

Power notes:

  • The Hi-Link 2W module's 400mA gives comfortable headroom over ESP32 Wi-Fi bursts, and 2W parts tolerate more output capacitance — the 1W startup-hiccup risk is gone. Still cap the bulk capacitance at ~470µF.
  • 105°C-rated capacitors matter here: inside a warm AC head unit, electrolytic lifespan roughly halves per 10°C. The upcharge over generic 85°C parts is ₹10–30 per cap — the cheapest longevity upgrade in the build.
  • The Mini560 is fixed-output — no trim pot to drift with temperature or get knocked out of adjustment (the reason we dropped the MP1584).
  • Use a WROOM-based DevKit, not WROVER — on WROVER, GPIO16/17 are taken by PSRAM.

With the ADuM1201 there's no inversion ambiguity — if the AC doesn't respond, check that TX/RX aren't swapped (AC pin 4 → VIA, VOB → AC pin 5).

6. Build & test sequence

  1. Verify the buck alone first. The Mini560 is fixed at 5V, but verify anyway: feed it 12–14V from a DC adapter with nothing on its output and confirm 5.0V out before wiring anything downstream.
  2. Bench-build. Assemble the perfboard. Before plugging in the ESP32: power the hot side from your 12V adapter and verify 5.0V on 5V_hot and ~5V on 5V_iso. Then confirm no continuity between GND_hot and GND_iso (meter in continuity mode, module unpowered).
  3. Plug in the ESP32, flash ESPHome over USB (module NOT connected to the AC), confirm it joins Wi-Fi and appears in Home Assistant (climate entity shows up, just no data yet).
  4. Run the harness voltage checks from section 2 (breaker off → harness in → breaker on → measure → breaker off, 10+ min wait). Only proceed if all three readings match.
  5. Connect the module to the harness: pins 1, 4, 5, 6 only. Position 10 in the housing stays empty. Triple-check you're counting from the +14V / ▲ end.
  6. Insulate the hot side: heat-shrink over the buck/opto perfboard section, module in the ABS box, box tucked inside the indoor unit housing (there's usually space near the control-box area). No exposed conductors.
  7. Power on. ESP32 boots from AC power, ESPHome logs (view wirelessly via esphome logs) should show S21 queries getting responses within seconds.
  8. Test from Home Assistant: mode, setpoint, fan speed. Verify the IR remote still works alongside.

7. Safety rules (non-negotiable)

  • The AC side of this circuit is at mains potential even when the compressor is "off". Only the breaker makes it safe, and only after the caps discharge.
  • Never plug USB into the ESP32 while the module is connected to the AC — even with isolation, don't tempt fate during debugging; use OTA/wireless logs instead.
  • Never work on the connector with power on.
  • If anything smells hot or the AC throws an error code after connection, breaker off, disconnect, re-check wiring.

8. Compatibility caveat

Your exact model (MTKL50UV16VA) isn't on Faikin's confirmed-units list, but a structurally identical setup (S403 direct connection) worked on other units, and your wiring diagram proves S403 is the designated Wi-Fi port. The ESPHome component is read-safe: if the protocol dialect differs, you'll simply see no/garbled responses in logs — nothing harmful. Faikin firmware also has protocol auto-detection if ESPHome's component doesn't match; hardware stays the same.

9. Optional add-on: IR blaster for the Orient Zeno fan

Your Zeno's remote is infrared (850nm) — confirmed from the remote's board: a single IR LED transmitter, no RF hardware. Its faint red glow (eye) / red flash (phone camera) is characteristic of 850nm emitters. Before soldering, run the definitive test once: 2–3 layers of black electrical tape over the LED dome should make the fan go deaf (tape blocks IR, passes radio).

Two ways to build the transmitter:

  1. Easy first pass (buy just the two items above the line): wire the ₹50 module's signal pin straight to GPIO26, VCC to 3V3, GND to GND_iso. No transistor, no extra resistor. Drive is ~20mA — your remote manages the room on far less, so this likely just works.
  2. Max-range upgrade (only if the module can't reach the fan): desolder the IR LED from the module and re-drive it through the 2N2222 per the schematic — base via 1kΩ from GPIO26, LED fed from 5V_iso through any 22–47Ω resistor (exact value non-critical; sets 70–150mA pulses). No need to hunt the TSAL6200.

Circuit — all on the cold (safe) side, no isolation changes:

  • IR TX: ESP32 GPIO26 → 1kΩ → S8050 base; emitter → GND_iso; collector → IR LED cathode; LED anode → 33Ω → 5V_iso. (~100mA pulsed — 20× your remote's drive, so wall reflections cover the room.)
  • IR RX: VS1838B — Vcc → 3V3, GND → GND_iso, OUT → GPIO27.
  • Mount the IR LED on a 20–30cm thin twin-wire pigtail poking out at the bottom edge of the AC's front panel, angled toward the fan. The receiver can stay on the board with a small window, or on its own short pigtail next to the LED.

ESPHome config additions:

remote_receiver:
  id: ir_rx
  pin:
    number: GPIO27
    inverted: true      # VS1838B output is active-low
  dump: all             # logs every code your remote sends

remote_transmitter:
  id: ir_tx
  pin: GPIO26
  carrier_duty_percent: 50%

Workflow: flash, open the ESPHome logs, press each button on the Zeno remote and note what dump: all prints (protocol + address/command, often NEC — or raw timings). Then add one template button per function, e.g.:

button:
  - platform: template
    name: "Zeno Speed 3"
    on_press:
      - remote_transmitter.transmit_nec:   # match whatever protocol the dump showed
          address: 0xEF00
          command: 0x18F7

If the dump shows raw instead of a named protocol, copy the timing list into remote_transmitter.transmit_raw with carrier_frequency: 38kHz. One-way control as always with IR: HA tracks fan state optimistically, and since the receiver stays installed, presses on the physical remote can update HA's state too.

Bonus: the same blaster can learn and send codes for any other IR device in that room (TV, soundbar, older ACs).
