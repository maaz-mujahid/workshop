1 · How it works

An AM312 mini PIR watches for body heat. Its output drives a 2N7000 MOSFET that switches 3 warm-white LEDs. A BC547 + LDR "daylight clamp" sits on the MOSFET gate: in daylight the transistor saturates and pins the gate to ground, so the LEDs can never fire. At night the clamp releases and the PIR has full control.

The AM312 holds its output ~2.3 s and retriggers continuously while you're in view — so the light comes on as you approach, stays on while you pass, and dies ~2–3 s after you're gone. Exactly the behaviour you asked for, with no code.

Standby draw is ~50–110 µA. A 2000 mAh 18650 gives roughly 4–6 months per USB-C charge at typical stair/kitchen traffic (~10 min of light per night).

3 · Salvaging the 18650s

Best sources, in order: dead laptop battery pack (6–9 cells; usually only 1–2 are actually bad — the BMS killed the pack, not the cells), old power bank, BT speaker, trimmer. Crack the pack open with a spudger, snip the nickel strips, and keep the strip stubs on the cells — solder to the stub, never long-heat the cell body.

Vet every cell before use:
  1. Look: reject anything rusty, dented, punctured, or that leaked.
  2. Voltage: measure open-circuit. ≥2.8 V → good candidate. 2.0–2.8 V → charge carefully, watch it. <2.0 V → bin it (copper shunts form; unsafe to revive).
  3. Self-discharge: charge to 4.2 V via TP4056, rest 3 days, re-measure. Dropped >0.1 V → reject.
  4. Warmth: if any cell gets more than mildly warm while charging → reject.
You need 5–6 keepers — start with ~10 candidates. Capacity will be 1000–1800 mAh instead of 2000; at ~9.5 mAh/day that's still 3–5 months per charge.
Never run a salvaged cell without the TP4056's protection side. The DW01 over-discharge cutoff (2.4 V) is the only thing standing between a tired cell and a swollen one. Always power the circuit from OUT+/OUT−.

4 · Circuit

Bright: LDR pulls BC547 base high → clamp saturates → gate held at GND → LEDs dead regardless of PIR. Dark: LDR goes high-resistance → clamp off → PIR OUT (3.3 V) drives the 2N7000 through the 1 kΩ.

Optional: soft fade-out

A 47 µF cap from gate node to GND makes the light fade out over ~2 s instead of snapping off (discharges through the 100 kΩ, MOSFET passes through its linear region). Purely cosmetic — try one unit first.

5 · Assembly

1. Charger + cell. Solder the salvaged cell's nickel-strip stubs to TP4056 B+/B− (flux + quick dab; never heat the cell body itself). From OUT+ go through the slide switch to the board's VBAT rail; OUT− is GND. Never bypass OUT± — that's your over-discharge protection.
2. LED bank. Three straw-hat LEDs, each with its own 47 Ω, in parallel: anodes (via resistors) to VBAT, cathodes to 2N7000 drain. Source to GND. ~75 mA at 4.2 V, ~45 mA at 3.7 V nominal — dims gracefully as the cell drains.
3. PIR. AM312 VCC→VBAT, GND→GND, OUT→1 kΩ→gate. 100 kΩ gate-to-GND pulldown.
4. Daylight clamp. LDR + 100 kΩ preset in series from VBAT to BC547 base; 47 kΩ base-to-GND; collector to gate node; emitter to GND. Set preset mid-way to start.
5. Enclosure. PIR dome through the front face. LDR through the top or rear, LEDs aimed down at the floor. USB-C port and switch accessible on the side.
Critical — LDR must not see its own LEDs. If the LDR catches the unit's own light it will read "daytime", cut the light, go dark, retrigger — a flicker loop. Point the LDR the opposite way from the LED throw and sleeve it in a short piece of heat-shrink as a snoot. Same rule at install time: keep it out of the direct pool of light on the floor.
Why the AM312 and not HC-SR501: the SR501 wants ≥4.5 V (its regulator drops out on a 3.7 V cell) and is 3× the size. The AM312 runs 2.7–12 V, draws <60 µA, and its fixed ~2.3 s retriggering hold is exactly the "on while I pass, off after" behaviour wanted here.
If the LEDs look dim on a fresh build: you got a high-threshold 2N7000 (Vgs(th) spec runs up to 3 V; the PIR gate drive is only 3.3 V). Swap in an AO3400 (SOT-23 on a breakout, ~₹5, robu) — fully on at 2.5 V. Order a strip of them with the cart as insurance.

6 · Mounting

  • Stairs (3–4 units): skirting height, ~30–60 cm off the treads — top landing, mid-flight, bottom. At ankle height the PIR sees legs the moment a foot enters the flight, and the low warm pool lights treads without blinding night-adapted eyes.
  • Kitchen (1–2 units): one at the entry aimed across the doorway, optionally one under a counter overhang aimed at the floor in front of the sink/stove run.
  • PIR is most sensitive to motion across its view, not straight at it — mount angled across the walking path.
  • Keep away from direct AC airflow and hot appliances (PIRs false-trigger on moving warm air).

7 · Test checklist (per unit, before closing the box)

  1. Switch on in a dark room → wave hand at 2–3 m → LEDs on, off ~2–3 s after you stop.
  2. Torch on the LDR → LEDs must refuse to fire even with motion. Adjust preset until room-light kills it but dusk doesn't.
  3. Cover the LDR with a finger while LEDs are lit → no flicker loop (confirms shielding).
  4. Charge test: plug USB-C (switch OFF) → red LED on TP4056, blue/green when full (~4.2 V across cell).
  5. Measure standby: multimeter in series on µA range, no motion, dark → expect ≤120 µA.

8 · Battery math

Per day: PIR ~50 µA × 24 h ≈ 1.2 mAh; LDR divider ≈ 0.8 mAh (mostly daytime); LEDs ~45 mA × 10 min ≈ 7.5 mAh. Total ≈ 9.5 mAh/day. Salvaged cells hold ~1000–1800 mAh; at 80% usable → ~3–5 months per charge (a strong salvage cell in a quiet stair unit will do 5+; a tired one in the kitchen maybe 10 weeks). Recharge = pop USB-C for 2–3 h. Put the best-tested cells in the highest-traffic spots.
